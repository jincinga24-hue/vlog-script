Analysis — Retrospective & Key Concepts
~40 sec
Through our team retrospective, reflecting on our Miro board — our SWOT analyses, archetype roles, and team charter — I was able to critically assess what happened. As a team, we agreed on two shared actions. PAUSE First, we committed to setting detailed milestones with specific deadlines — because honestly, most of us have a tendency to leave things until later, and without hard checkpoints we were drifting. Second, when two people are assigned to the same task, we agreed to clearly define who owns which part. Kim and I learned this the hard way — during customer research, we both independently researched the same set of prospects and ended up with duplicate work, which cost us time we didn't have. That experience made it clear: shared responsibility without clear division just means wasted effort. PAUSE

This connects to what we learned in the High-Performing Teams Masterclass about Radical Candor — Kim Scott's framework where high-performing teams need to both care personally and challenge directly. PAUSE We were entering what Tuckman calls the storming stage, and I experienced this directly. An wanted to incorporate content from Masterclass 2 into our team retrospective — and part of me wanted to just say yes, because I could see how much she cared about making the retro stronger. But the timeline was already tight, and I knew from my SWOT that my weakness is overcommitting — saying yes to everything until quality drops. CAMERA So I raised the concern. I told An I thought adding more content would stretch us too thin and risk the quality of what we'd already prepared. That was hard for me — my sensitivity means I worry about how people react, and I didn't want her to feel shut down. PAUSE But that moment was my turning point — moving from what Kim Scott calls Ruinous Empathy, where you care but don't challenge, to Radical Candor, where you care and you're honest. As a Giver, my value isn't just in being supportive. It's in being supportive and honest.

TONE The prospect research challenge reinforced this shift. Chris asked us to find 100 high-potential prospect contacts — decision-maker names, emails, and LinkedIn profiles for companies likely to convert. We started doing it manually — searching LinkedIn one by one, cross-referencing company websites. SLOW After half an hour, we had five. At that rate, it would have taken us days. So after discussing it as a team, I went and learned how to use Claude to automate LinkedIn prospecting — pulling company data, matching it against our target segments, and generating structured contact lists. We hit 100 contacts in a fraction of the time.

If I could do it again, I would have spoken up immediately in that meeting with Chris, rather than processing it quietly. PAUSE But what I did do was bring it to the team — and we had a productive discussion about how to navigate the ACL and greenwashing risks while still communicating Proskill's genuine environmental impact. That developed a new capability in me — balancing support with honest challenge.

SLOW So I want to START being more upfront about challenges in the moment. In consulting, early honesty saves time.

PAUSE

On the prospect research — rather than accepting the manual approach, I took the initiative to learn how to use Claude integrated with LinkedIn to automate the whole process. I didn't know how to do it before — I figured it out because the team needed it. CAMERA That shift — from supporting the team emotionally to also technically — is a capability I want to carry into my professional career. In consulting, self-directed learning isn't optional — clients don't wait for you to be ready.

PAUSE

I want to STOP letting my effectiveness fade over time. My SWOT identified this — I start strong but that energy can drop off. To manage this, I'm setting myself a weekly self-review — checking whether my contributions that week match the standard I set in week one.

TONE And I'll CONTINUE investing in team trust. Our retrospective showed that the reason we navigated these challenges was because we had psychological safety — and as the Giver, that's my contribution.
